#4 Currency pairs: majors, minors, and exotics

In the forex market, currencies are always traded in pairs. This is because you're simultaneously buying one currency and selling another. For example, if you're trading the EUR/USD pair, you're buying euros while selling U.S. dollars (or vice versa).

Currency pairs are generally grouped into three main categories: majors, minors, and exotics. Each group has distinct characteristics in terms of liquidity, volatility, and trading costs.

1. Major Currency Pairs

Major pairs are the most commonly traded currency pairs in the world. They all include the U.S. dollar (USD) on one side, paired with another major global currency. These pairs are the most liquid, meaning they have the highest trading volume and tightest spreads.

Examples of Major Pairs:

  • EUR/USD – Euro / U.S. Dollar (most traded pair globally)
  • GBP/USD – British Pound / U.S. Dollar
  • USD/JPY – U.S. Dollar / Japanese Yen
  • USD/CHF – U.S. Dollar / Swiss Franc
  • AUD/USD – Australian Dollar / U.S. Dollar
  • USD/CAD – U.S. Dollar / Canadian Dollar
  • NZD/USD – New Zealand Dollar / U.S. Dollar

Key Features:

  • High liquidity and tighter spreads
  • Lower trading costs
  • Heavily influenced by economic data from the U.S. and other major economies
  • Preferred by both beginners and professionals

2. Minor Currency Pairs (Cross Currency Pairs)

Minor pairs, also known as cross currency pairs, do not include the U.S. dollar. These are combinations of major currencies, such as the euro, pound, yen, or Australian dollar.

Examples of Minor Pairs:

  • EUR/GBP – Euro / British Pound
  • EUR/JPY – Euro / Japanese Yen
  • GBP/JPY – British Pound / Japanese Yen
  • AUD/NZD – Australian Dollar / New Zealand Dollar

Key Features:

  • Slightly lower liquidity than major pairs
  • Spreads are generally wider than majors
  • Often more volatile due to fewer traders and less volume
  • Still relatively popular among experienced traders

3. Exotic Currency Pairs

Exotic pairs consist of one major currency paired with a currency from a developing or emerging market economy. These pairs are less liquid, more volatile, and often come with significantly higher spreads and slippage.

Examples of Exotic Pairs:

  • USD/TRY – U.S. Dollar / Turkish Lira
  • USD/THB – U.S. Dollar / Thai Baht
  • EUR/ZAR – Euro / South African Rand
  • GBP/SGD – British Pound / Singapore Dollar
  • USD/MYR – U.S. Dollar / Malaysian Ringgit

Key Features:

  • Low liquidity, high spreads
  • Increased risk due to political instability or economic unpredictability in emerging markets
  • Potential for high rewards, but also higher risk of losses
  • More suitable for advanced traders with proper risk management strategies

Why Currency Pairs Matter

Understanding the type of currency pair you're trading is essential because it affects:

  • Liquidity and volatility
  • Trading strategy
  • Risk management
  • News impact and fundamental analysis

Beginners often start with major pairs due to their predictability and cost-efficiency. As traders gain more experience, they may explore minors and exotics to capitalize on greater volatility and diversification.
