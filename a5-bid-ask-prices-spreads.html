#5 Bid/Ask Prices and Spreads in Forex Trading

To understand how forex trading works, it’s essential to grasp the concepts of bid price, ask price, and the spread. These terms determine how much you pay to enter and exit a trade, and they directly affect your potential profitability.

1. What Is the Bid Price?

The bid price is the price at which the market (or broker) is willing to buy a currency pair from you. In other words, this is the price you’ll receive if you want to sell the base currency in the pair.

Example: In the EUR/USD pair, if the bid price is 1.1000, that means you can sell 1 euro for 1.1000 U.S. dollars.

2. What Is the Ask Price?

The ask price (also known as the offer price) is the price at which the market is willing to sell a currency pair to you. This is the price you’ll pay if you want to buy the base currency.

Example: If the ask price for EUR/USD is 1.1003, it means you have to pay 1.1003 U.S. dollars to buy 1 euro.

3. What Is the Spread?

The spread is the difference between the ask price and the bid price. This is essentially the cost of trading and is how most brokers make money—especially those that don’t charge a commission.

Spread Formula:
 Spread = Ask Price – Bid Price

Using our example:
 Ask: 1.1003
 Bid: 1.1000
 Spread = 0.0003, or 3 pips

4. What Is a Pip?

A pip (short for "percentage in point") is the standard unit of measurement in forex trading, usually equal to 0.0001 for most currency pairs. It represents the smallest price move that a currency pair can make based on market convention.

So, a spread of 3 pips means you're paying 0.0003 of the quote currency per unit traded.

5. Fixed vs Variable Spreads

  • Fixed Spreads: Remain constant regardless of market conditions. Usually offered by market maker brokers. Good for beginners, but can be slightly higher.
  • Variable (Floating) Spreads: Change depending on market volatility and liquidity. Lower during calm periods, but can widen significantly during major news events. Common with ECN/STP brokers.

6. How Spreads Affect Your Trades

When you open a trade, you’re immediately in a small loss equal to the spread. Your trade needs to move in your favor by at least the size of the spread before you can start making a profit.

The tighter the spread, the cheaper it is to trade—especially for short-term strategies like scalping or day trading.

7. Factors That Influence Spreads

  • Currency pair liquidity (Majors = tighter spreads, Exotics = wider spreads)
  • Time of day (Spreads are tighter during major sessions like London/New York overlap)
  • Market volatility (Spreads may widen during economic news releases or unexpected events)
  • Broker type (ECN/STP brokers often offer tighter, more transparent spreads)

Conclusion

Understanding bid, ask, and spread prices is crucial for calculating your trade costs and managing risk. Even small spreads can add up over time, especially for frequent traders. Always choose a broker that offers competitive spreads and transparent pricing.
